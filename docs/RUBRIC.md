# LUCID Manipulation Rubric

This document defines the 6 dimensions of psychological manipulation that LUCID scores in short-form video content. Each dimension is grounded in peer-reviewed research and operationalized for both LLM-as-judge labeling and human validation.

**Status:** draft — awaiting Lindsay's T&S review / voice edits
**Scoring scale:** 0 (absent) · 1 (moderate) · 2 (severe) · per dimension
**Composite Scroll Trap Score:** normalized sum of 6 dimensions → 0–100

---

## 1. Outrage Bait

### Academic grounding
- Crockett, M. J. (2017). Moral outrage in the digital age. *Nature Human Behaviour*, 1(11), 769–771. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-017-0213-3
- Brady, W. J., Wills, J. A., Jost, J. T., Tucker, J. A., & Van Bavel, J. J. (2017). Emotion shapes the diffusion of moralized content in social networks. *PNAS*, 114(28), 7313–7318. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1618923114
- Brady, W. J., McLoughlin, K., Doan, T. N., & Crockett, M. J. (2021). How social learning amplifies moral outrage expression in online social networks. *Science Advances*, 7(33), eabe5641. https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.abe5641

### Mechanism
Moralized and outrage-evoking content activates tribal in-group/out-group cognition and is shared as a costly signal of moral commitment; platform reward signals reinforce increasingly extreme expression via social learning. Each moral-emotional word has been shown to increase diffusion by ~20% per word within ideological networks (Brady et al. 2017).

### Rubric definition
Content whose framing is primarily designed to elicit anger, moral indignation, contempt, or tribal ("us vs. them") reaction rather than to inform or persuade on the merits. Signals include moralized language ("disgusting," "evil," "betrayal"), villain framing of an out-group, moral-violation hooks without context, and rhetorical questions that assume the audience's outrage. Score based on whether outrage is the dominant rhetorical goal — not whether the underlying claim is true.

### Severity examples
- **0 (absent):** "New city council ordinance passed today on short-term rentals. Here's a summary of what changed."
- **1 (moderate):** "Wait until you see what this landlord just tried to pull on a single mom. Absolutely shameful behavior."
- **2 (severe):** "DISGUSTING. These people are laughing in your face while they rob you blind. If you're not ANGRY right now you're part of the problem."

## 2. FOMO Trigger

### Academic grounding
- Przybylski, A. K., Murayama, K., DeHaan, C. R., & Gladwell, V. (2013). Motivational, emotional, and behavioral correlates of fear of missing out. *Computers in Human Behavior*, 29(4), 1841–1848. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chb.2013.02.014
- Cialdini, R. B. (2009). *Influence: Science and Practice* (5th ed.). Pearson.
- Tandon, A., Dhir, A., Almugren, I., AlNemer, G. N., & Mäntymäki, M. (2021). Fear of missing out (FoMO) among social media users: a systematic literature review. *Internet Research*, 31(3), 782–821. https://doi.org/10.1108/INTR-11-2019-0455

### Mechanism
FOMO is a self-regulatory deficit in which perceived absence from rewarding experiences others are having produces anxiety, driving compulsive checking and conformity (Przybylski et al. 2013). Scarcity and social-proof cues (Cialdini 2009) further exploit loss aversion and normative conformity to short-circuit deliberation.

### Rubric definition
Content that manufactures urgency, exclusivity, or social comparison to induce anxiety about being left behind or left out. Indicators include scarcity framing ("only X left," "last chance"), bandwagon claims ("everyone is doing this"), exclusivity claims ("most people don't know"), and comparative lifestyle displays used to destabilize the viewer's status. Score based on whether the content *weaponizes* scarcity or social comparison rather than merely describing a time-bound event.

### Severity examples
- **0 (absent):** "Signups for the local 5K close on Friday — link in bio if you want to run."
- **1 (moderate):** "Everyone I know is already using this app and I feel like I'm behind. Don't be the last one to find out."
- **2 (severe):** "ONLY 3 SPOTS LEFT. Every successful 22-year-old is already inside this program. If you're still watching this video instead of clicking, you're going to be stuck exactly where you are for the next 10 years."

## 3. Engagement Bait

### Academic grounding
- Meta Newsroom. (2017, December 18). *Fighting Engagement Bait on Facebook*. https://about.fb.com/news/2017/12/news-feed-fyi-fighting-engagement-bait-on-facebook/
- Munger, K. (2020). All the news that's fit to click: The economics of clickbait media. *Political Communication*, 37(3), 376–397. https://doi.org/10.1080/10584609.2019.1687626
- Mathur, A., Acar, G., Friedman, M. J., Lucherini, E., Mayer, J., Chetty, M., & Narayanan, A. (2019). Dark patterns at scale: Findings from a crawl of 11K shopping websites. *Proceedings of the ACM on HCI*, 3(CSCW), 1–32. https://doi.org/10.1145/3359183

### Mechanism
Platform ranking algorithms historically used engagement signals (comments, shares, tags, reactions) as proxies for content quality, so creators learned to issue explicit interaction prompts that game distribution rather than signal value (Meta 2017; Munger 2020). This is a form of interface-level dark pattern that extracts behavior from users without a corresponding informational payoff (Mathur et al. 2019).

### Rubric definition
Content that explicitly solicits a low-cost user action (comment, tag, share, follow, "say yes") whose primary function is to inflate algorithmic signals rather than to facilitate genuine discussion. Signals include "tag a friend who…," "comment X if you agree," "share if you love your mom," "follow for part 2." Score based on whether the solicitation is the content's main call to action and whether the interaction is contrived rather than topically motivated.

### Severity examples
- **0 (absent):** "I've been experimenting with sourdough for a month — happy to answer questions in the comments if anyone's curious."
- **1 (moderate):** "Tag a friend who needs to see this. It might change their week."
- **2 (severe):** "COMMENT 'YES' IF YOU LOVE YOUR MOM. Share to 3 stories or you don't REALLY care. Follow for part 2, 3, 4, and 5."

## 4. Emotional Manipulation

### Academic grounding
- Cialdini, R. B., Schaller, M., Houlihan, D., Arps, K., Fultz, J., & Beaman, A. L. (1987). Empathy-based helping: Is it selflessly or selfishly motivated? *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 52(4), 749–758. https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.52.4.749
- Small, D. A., Loewenstein, G., & Slovic, P. (2007). Sympathy and callousness: The impact of deliberative thought on donations to identifiable and statistical victims. *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*, 102(2), 143–153. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.obhdp.2006.01.005
- Kramer, A. D. I., Guillory, J. E., & Hancock, J. T. (2014). Experimental evidence of massive-scale emotional contagion through social networks. *PNAS*, 111(24), 8788–8790. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1320040111

### Mechanism
Guilt and empathy-based appeals leverage the identifiable-victim effect and social-obligation norms to pressure prosocial compliance even when action is unwarranted or the claim is unverified (Small, Loewenstein & Slovic 2007; Cialdini et al. 1987). Emotional contagion in social feeds further amplifies affective responses without requiring in-person contact (Kramer, Guillory & Hancock 2014).

### Rubric definition
Content that leverages guilt, pity, empathy, shame, or moral obligation to coerce a response (sharing, donating, engaging, believing) rather than providing evidence or argument. Indicators include guilt-trip framing ("if you scroll past, you don't care"), identifiable-victim appeals used without context or verification, and implicit moral tests of the viewer's character. Score based on whether emotional pressure *substitutes* for justification — not merely whether the content evokes emotion.

### Severity examples
- **0 (absent):** "Our local shelter is at capacity. If you're in a position to foster, the link is in bio."
- **1 (moderate):** "This little guy has been at the shelter for 147 days. I can't stop thinking about him. Please share so he finds a home."
- **2 (severe):** "99% of people will scroll past this. If you have ANY heart at all, you'll share this to your story. If you don't, we both know what kind of person you really are."

## 5. Curiosity Gap

### Academic grounding
- Loewenstein, G. (1994). The psychology of curiosity: A review and reinterpretation. *Psychological Bulletin*, 116(1), 75–98. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.116.1.75
- Blom, J. N., & Hansen, K. R. (2015). Click bait: Forward-reference as lure in online news headlines. *Journal of Pragmatics*, 76, 87–100. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pragma.2014.11.010
- Scott, K. (2021). You won't believe what's in this paper! Clickbait, relevance and the curiosity gap. *Journal of Pragmatics*, 175, 53–66. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pragma.2020.12.023

### Mechanism
Curiosity is an aversive state produced by an information gap between what one knows and what one wants to know; resolving the gap is rewarding, which creators exploit by deliberately withholding a specific referent (Loewenstein 1994). Forward-referential cataphora ("the reason will shock you," "wait for it") defers the payoff specifically to force click-through or watch-through (Blom & Hansen 2015; Scott 2021). Related: Zeigarnik effect — unresolved tasks remain cognitively intrusive.

### Rubric definition
Content that deliberately withholds a key referent, outcome, or explanation to compel the viewer to click, scroll, or watch to completion. Signals include forward-referring pronouns without antecedents ("THIS is why…"), suspense markers ("wait for it…," "…and then THIS happened"), and vague superlatives ("the one thing nobody tells you about X"). Score based on whether the information withholding is *structural to the hook* rather than a natural narrative tease.

### Severity examples
- **0 (absent):** "Here's how I cut my grocery bill by 30%: I switched to store-brand staples and started meal-prepping on Sundays."
- **1 (moderate):** "I tried a new morning routine for 30 days and the results surprised me — full breakdown below."
- **2 (severe):** "You won't BELIEVE what she pulled out of the drawer… WAIT FOR IT… (the ending is insane, watch till the end)"

## 6. Dopamine Design

### Academic grounding
- Skinner, B. F. (1953). *Science and Human Behavior*. Macmillan.
- Alter, A. (2017). *Irresistible: The Rise of Addictive Technology and the Business of Keeping Us Hooked*. Penguin Press.
- Montag, C., Lachmann, B., Herrlich, M., & Zweig, K. (2019). Addictive features of social media/messenger platforms and freemium games against the background of psychological and economic theories. *International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health*, 16(14), 2612. https://doi.org/10.3390/ijerph16142612
- Lupinacci, L. (2021). "Absentmindedly scrolling through nothing": Liveness and compulsory continuous connectedness in social media. *Media, Culture & Society*, 43(2), 273–290. https://doi.org/10.1177/0163443720939454

### Mechanism
Variable-ratio reinforcement schedules produce the most persistent, compulsive response patterns in operant conditioning (Skinner 1953), a principle that social-feed and short-video interfaces operationalize through unpredictable high-salience stimuli and pull-to-refresh mechanics (Alter 2017; Montag et al. 2019). At the post level, surface-feature saturation — ALL CAPS, dense emoji, rapid cuts, bright color grading, high-arousal audio — functions as a low-cost salience hook that captures attention before cognitive evaluation.

### Rubric definition
Surface-level attention-capture tactics operating at the formatting, typography, and audiovisual layer rather than the semantic layer. Indicators include sustained ALL CAPS, excessive punctuation (!!!, ???), emoji spamming, rapid jump cuts, high-saturation color/filter effects, loud or trending audio layered for arousal, and variable-reward structural devices like numbered hook lists or rapid reveal cycles. Score based on the *density and intentionality* of surface salience hooks, independent of the content's underlying claims.

### Severity examples
- **0 (absent):** "Three things I learned from my first year of teaching. One: lesson plans never survive first contact with a classroom." *(calm delivery, normal caps, single voice)*
- **1 (moderate):** "Okay so you NEED to hear this!! 3 things that changed my life this year (number 2 is wild)" *(some caps, one emoji cluster, moderate cuts)*
- **2 (severe):** "WAIT WAIT WAIT!!! STOP SCROLLING!!! YOU NEED TO SEE THIS!!! [rapid zoom] [trending audio spike] [jump cut every 0.4s] [red arrows, yellow text, shaking emojis]"

## Inter-annotator validation protocol

To establish the credibility of our LLM-as-judge labeling at scale, we validate against a human-labeled gold set:

1. **Gold set construction:** Lindsay hand-labels 100 randomly sampled items from the corpus across all 6 dimensions using this rubric.
2. **LLM labeling:** Claude Sonnet 4.5 labels the full corpus (~2,000 items) using this rubric as a system prompt with few-shot examples.
3. **Agreement analysis:** Per-dimension Spearman correlation and Krippendorff's α (ordinal) between Lindsay's and Claude's labels on the 100-post gold set.
4. **Second-model sanity check:** GPT-4 labels the same 100-post gold set; report Claude vs. GPT agreement to characterize task-inherent noise.
5. **Reporting:** All agreement numbers, per dimension, appear in the technical report — strong or weak.

This protocol follows current practice for LLM-as-judge evaluation (e.g., scalable oversight literature from Anthropic and others) and acknowledges that our labels are an approximation of expert judgment, not ground truth.
